Setup rationale

Why PDH / PDL works

  • Prior-day extremes are visible liquidity pools. Stops often cluster just outside them.
  • When price raids PDH or PDL and rejects, trapped breakout traders fuel the reversal.
  • When price breaks and then holds the level, it shows acceptance and often expands further.
  • The level gives a clean invalidation point, which keeps risk measurable rather than emotional.

Previous Day High / Previous Day Low playbook

PDH and PDL are not magic lines. They are reference points where liquidity tends to sit. The edge comes from reading how price behaves when it reaches those zones: rejection, acceptance, or a stop run that snaps back.

1

Mark the range

Plot yesterday's high and low before London opens. Ignore noise; respect the full session range.

2

Wait for interaction

Let price reach PDH or PDL. The level itself matters less than the reaction around it.

3

Choose the model

Trade the rejection if the sweep fails. Trade continuation if the break holds and retests cleanly.

4

Define invalidation

Stop goes beyond the raid or failed retest. If that level breaks again, your idea is wrong.

Risk / disclaimer

Clear language for a trading audience: precise, realistic, and not promotional.

Risk comes first

  • This dashboard is educational and illustrative. It is not financial advice, investment advice, or a promise of results.
  • Markets can gap, slip, and overshoot prior-day levels. PDH / PDL reactions fail often enough that every trade must be pre-sized.
  • Never risk money you cannot afford to lose. One good setup does not justify oversized risk.
  • Use hard invalidation, respect news events, and avoid forcing trades when price is trapped in the middle of the range.
  • If you cannot explain where your stop goes and why, you do not have a valid setup yet.
